San Francisco stands out: Vacancy surged from 8.7% to 11.9% (+3.2pp) and rental vacancy nearly doubled from 2.9% to 6.1% (+3.2pp) — the largest increase among major urban counties, driven by pandemic-era remote work and new housing supply. San Mateo saw a similar pattern with rental vacancy spiking from 3.6% to 7.7% (+4.1pp). Meanwhile, Inland Empire counties (Riverside, San Bernardino) saw vacancy drop sharply as affordable housing seekers moved east — Riverside fell from 13.8% to 11.4% (-2.4pp).
Statewide Pattern: California's overall vacancy rate barely moved (8.0% → 7.6%), but this hides two diverging stories. Bay Area/coastal tech hubs (SF, San Mateo, Alameda, Santa Clara) saw vacancy rise significantly — more empty units, especially rentals — reflecting the post-pandemic remote work shift. Inland/affordable areas (Riverside, San Bernardino, Sacramento, Fresno, Kern) saw vacancy fall as demand surged from people priced out of coastal metros or seeking space. Resort/mountain counties (Alpine, Mono, Sierra) saw the biggest absolute drops, as second homes became primary residences.
