Methodology

Data pipeline: All primary data was pulled directly from the U.S. Census Bureau API (api.census.gov) and BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics files (bls.gov/oes/special-requests). No third-party aggregators were used for the core housing and employment data.

Vacancy trends: We pulled ACS 5-Year estimates for 6 consecutive years (2018–2023) for every county, computing vacancy rate (vacant ÷ total units) and rental vacancy rate (for-rent vacant ÷ (renter-occupied + for-rent vacant)) for each year. The 5-year change in percentage points determines whether a market is tightening or loosening.

Rent-to-cost ratio: Compares B25064 (median gross rent) to B25105 (median monthly owner cost for units with a mortgage). A ratio ≥ 1.0 means median rent exceeds median owner cost — indicating potential for Day 1 positive cash flow on acquired multifamily properties. This is a proxy; actual deal-level economics depend on acquisition price, financing terms, capex, and management costs.

Scoring model: Counties were scored on a 100-point scale: vacancy trend (25pts), rental vacancy trend (15pts), rent/cost ratio (30pts — weighted highest per fund criteria), population growth (15pts), multifamily stock depth (10pts), and market size/liquidity (5pts). Only counties with 50K+ housing units and 10K+ renter households were included.

Multifamily stock: Derived from B25024 (units in structure), summing 3-4 unit, 5-9, 10-19, 20-49, and 50+ unit buildings. Markets with deeper existing multifamily stock offer better acquisition pipelines and property management infrastructure.

Market intelligence: Cap rates, rent growth projections, and supply pipeline data were sourced from Colliers, Yardi Matrix, NorthMarq, Marcus & Millichap, Matthews, and other commercial real estate research firms via their publicly available reports.
